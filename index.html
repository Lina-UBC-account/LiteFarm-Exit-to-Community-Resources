iNaturalist

www.inaturalist.org

Pathway B.1

iNaturalist is a large citizen-science platform for biodiversity monitoring, which started as a grad school project and then was nurtured by a series of non-profit institutions. From 2014 to 2023 iNaturalist was jointly sponsored by the California Academy of Sciences and the National Geographic Society. In 2023, iNaturalist spun off into an independent 501(c)(3) non-profit to govern itself. Governance is now vested in a dedicated board  that includes conservation tech experts and community representatives, and an executive team. Decision-making remains top-down in practice (core staff decide on features and policies) but with transparency and community input mechanisms (forums for suggestions, open-source code for visibility). The community (over 2 million users) contributes data and helps with species identifications, but governance of the platform (e.g. setting community guidelines, feature priorities) lies with the non-profit’s leadership. iNaturalist explicitly notes that “being community-driven” for them means being guided by users’ needs, though not controlled by user votes, similar to a benevolent governance model.

iNaturalist has been largely grant-funded and donor-funded. As of 2023, most revenue comes from philanthropies (e.g. Moore Foundation) and donations. It also enjoys in-kind support (like cloud credits from tech companies) and a passionate user base that donates funds. This funding model – heavy reliance on philanthropy – has pros (stability when large grants are secured, e.g. a $10M grant in 2021) and cons (less diversified income). The new non-profit is continuing to cultivate grants and exploring new funding to support growth. Importantly, iNaturalist kept the platform free to use to maximize community participation, which aligns with its mission as a public science commons. Sustainability therefore depends on convincing funders of its public value.

iNaturalist’s codebase is open source (MIT license) and available on GitHub, mainly for transparency and enabling volunteer contributions. However, unlike many OSS projects, iNaturalist does not encourage independent forks or third-party instances – the focus is on one global platform (the contributing guide notes they code for a single instance and may not accept changes aimed at custom forks). Data contributed are under open licenses (users can choose licensing for their observations), fostering scientific reuse. This approach shows how open licensing can be used to build trust and community contributions even if the project is service-oriented.

For LiteFarm, iNaturalist demonstrates an academic project scaling up via institutional support and then spinning out. A challenge they faced was aligning two big sponsors (California Academy of Sciences museum and NatGeo); independence simplified governance, giving iNaturalist a singular focus. It also underscores that being “community-driven” can mean prioritizing user needs without handing over formal control. iNaturalist engages its users in feature discussions but retains a clear leadership structure. The case study is a lesson on the importance of long-term host institutions: Cal Academy and NatGeo’s incubation (providing staff, tech, and legitimacy) was crucial until iNaturalist could stand alone. Now as an independent non-profit, maintaining financial sustainability (post-grant) and scaling governance are new challenges. They plan to keep collaborating with their international network (many countries have local iNaturalist partnerships), which highlights the need for global community governance models when a project’s user base is worldwide.

Ghost

www.ghost.org

Pathway B.1

Ghost is an open-source content management system used by professional writers and creators to build websites, manage memberships, and send email newsletters. It is governed by the Ghost Foundation, a non-profit organization established by the founder (John O’Nolan) when Ghost launched. The foundation’s charter ensures the project can never be bought or controlled by shareholders as it’s legally structured to prioritize users and the open-source mission. Governance is effectively a benevolent dictatorship within a non-profit: O’Nolan and a small board make strategic decisions, but they are constrained by the non-profit’s mission. There is no formal community council deciding features; instead, the core team (employed by the foundation) sets the roadmap, informed by user feedback. However, Ghost is very transparent about its operations (they publish financials and metrics openly). This model has worked because the foundation’s goals (grow the product for users, not profit) align with the community’s interest. Ghost has a sustainable open-source business model centered on providing a hosted service Ghost(Pro).

All software is free and MIT-licensed, but users pay for the convenience of managed hosting. This has generated steady revenue – over $8 million from customers as of 2021, with Ghost profitable every year since its 2013 Kickstarter. Uniquely, because Ghost is a non-profit, 100% of revenue is reinvested into the project and community, rather than taken as profit. This virtuous cycle (“the more people use Ghost, the more demand for the paid service… the more great people we can hire… the better the software gets”) is explicitly the strategy. Ghost’s upfront crowdfunding and later subscription model provide financial independence with no equity or venture capital involved. They also diversify with optional premium features (Ghost has built-in memberships for publishers to earn money, which indirectly encourages growth of the platform).

Ghost is released under the MIT License – a permissive license. This choice made adoption easy (no copyleft concerns) and allowed even competitors or third parties to use the code. The Ghost Foundation’s view is that the code being free is non-negotiable; their competitive edge is in service. By being permissive and a non-profit, they attract goodwill and contributions (though most development is done by paid staff). They deliberately avoided a dual-license or open-core approach – all features land in the open product, which is a philosophical stance that loyalty of users will drive them to support Ghost(Pro) if the product is valuable.

Ghost’s success highlights non-profit stewardship with a revenue stream. For LiteFarm, this suggests one possible exit path: create a foundation and offer value-added services (e.g. host LiteFarm for a fee) to fund development. The key lessons from Ghost are: 

●                Independence: Staying free of external investors kept the project’s goals aligned with users (Ghost can’t be pressured to pivot or monetize user data, building huge trust). 

●                Transparency: Publishing live financial data and metrics builds community confidence.

●                Focus on core mission: Ghost is laser-focused on publishing tools, saying no to unrelated feature creep – this clarity helps governance. A potential pitfall Ghost managed to avoid was revenue shortfall; by introducing paid hosting early, they avoided the fate of many OSS projects that rely purely on donations.

Ghost shows that an open-source product can generate millions in revenue as a non-profit, if it identifies a service users will happily pay for and governs itself in a way that prioritizes trust and long-term stability.

Open Food Network

www.openfoodnetwork.org​

Pathway B.1

Open Food Network is an open-source platform and global community for local food systems. Its governance is highly collaborative and federated. Founded in Australia (2012) as a non-profit (Open Food Foundation), it now operates in ~20 countries via local affiliates. The global governance model is open and transparent, organized in circles, and a collective decision process. Each country instance is usually a non-profit or cooperative that runs an OFN deployment and contributes back. Globally, they have a “global community pledge” and a Governance & Finance circle that coordinates strategy and resource sharing across instances. This circle includes representatives from various country communities and reports to the board of the Open Food Foundation in AUS (which provides legal oversight). Decision-making strives for consent-based approaches: for example, feature priorities are decided in community discussions, and development is distributed among teams in different countries. In practice, OFN’s core dev team (spread internationally) handles technical decisions day-to-day, but larger changes (governance rules, major roadmap themes) are made collectively in community meetings. This “collective of affiliates” governance means no single entity owns OFN – it’s managed as a commons. They explicitly say the software and resources belong to the community, not any one organization. This has kept governance aligned with diverse user needs (farmers, food hubs on multiple continents) and avoided dominance by one government or company.

OFN’s funding is likewise distributed. Each local instance finds its own sustainable model (often a mix of grants, government funding, and service revenue). Many instances charge small fees for using the platform (e.g. a percentage on sales or a subscription for food hubs), which sustain their local operations. Globally, the Open Food Foundation and partners have secured grants from philanthropic sources. During COVID-19, usage spiked and some governments funded OFN deployments to support local food distribution. The global OFN network shares fundraising leads and often collaborates on grant proposals (with one country taking lead and others as partners). Some income also comes from tech incubator programs and social enterprise awards. This multi-stream funding has its challenges (not a single business model, but patchwork), yet it provides resilience – if one source dries up in one region, others can pick up slack. The community collectively invests back into development: e.g. if France’s OFN pays a developer, that code benefits all instances. All finances are openly tracked, and the global circle allocates pooled resources (like deciding how to fund core devs who work for all). In summary, OFN’s sustainability approach is “networked self-sufficiency”: each node of the network seeks sustainability, and contributes to the core pot for shared needs.

OFN’s software is open source under AGPL v3. This strong copyleft license fits their values of common ownership – it ensures that improvements by any deployer must be shared globally. As a result, even commercial or government users who might modify OFN for their region have to contribute back, preventing fragmentation. All documentation and knowledge resources are also shared openly (often Creative Commons licensed). By holding everything in the commons, OFN enables deep collaboration: a new country can spin up an instance without paying license fees, and they benefit from all prior development. This licensing strategy cements the idea that no affiliate can privatize the core software, which has kept the network unified. OFN’s model shows how cooperative-aligned governance can scale internationally.

A lesson for LiteFarm is the power of forming a coalition of like-minded organizations to steward a project. Rather than one organization trying to serve the world on its own, OFN created a framework where many local groups take ownership, which might be applicable if LiteFarm engages regional ag partners or NGOs. Governance-wise, this distributed model requires excellent communication and trust – OFN invests in community management and uses tools like Discourse forums and regular online meetings to keep everyone aligned. One challenge has been decision speed; collaborative decision-making can be slow, but it often leads to more buy-in. They mitigate issues by having circles with clear domains (e.g. a Product circle, a Tech circle) operating under sociocratic principles. Another challenge is funding the “global” needs (platform improvements, maintenance) which aren’t tied to one country – OFN handles this by having wealthier or more active instances contribute more, and through grants specifically for core development.

The lesson for sustainability is to embed the project in the ecosystem it serves: OFN is used by food co-ops and farmers who believe in it, some of whom become volunteers, staff, or funders. That community passion is as important as formal structure. For LiteFarm, building a strong user community and possibly a consortium of supporting orgs (like OFN’s affiliates) could distribute the load of both governance and funding. OFN’s story also illustrates the concept of “digital public infrastructure”: treat the software as a shared public service and seek public or charitable funding aligned with that (many OFN grants frame it as improving local food resilience). In conclusion, Open Food Network demonstrates a successful federated governance model where community ownership, transparency, and common licensing have enabled it to thrive across borders without sacrificing its mission.

Godot Engine 

www.godotengine.org

Pathway B.1

Godot is a free, open-source game engine governed by its community of contributors, with a structure that has evolved as the project grew. Initially, Godot had two primary maintainers (the founders) who acted as Benevolent Dictators, but over time it adopted a more community-driven process while retaining core leadership. Currently, governance can be described as a meritocratic core team with Benevolent Dictatorship For Life (BDFL) tendencies. There is no formal foundation board dictating technical decisions; instead, trusted maintainers (drawn from top contributors) have decision-making power over different subsystems. Final say on major changes often rests with the lead maintainer, Juan Linietsky, for engine architecture, for example. However, all design discussions, proposals, and progress are done in the open on GitHub and QA forums, and anyone can submit proposals. The term “community-driven” in Godot’s context means the project’s direction is guided by community needs (features are prioritized if they solve real users’ problems) but not literally driven by majority vote of users. The core maintainers exercise editorial control to keep quality and coherence. Juan explicitly stated that calling Godot community-driven “puts community interests in front, but doesn’t mean there is democracy or design-by-committee”: they favor a small group of decision-makers for efficiency. Yet, they do listen: there’s a proposal system where ideas get community feedback and must show broad interest before implementation. This hybrid governance has worked well: it prevented forks and in-fighting by having clear leadership, while still incorporating community contributions (hundreds of volunteer contributors for engine improvements, documentation, demos, etc). In 2020, they formed the Godot Foundation to support the project, but that foundation supports fundraising and hiring, not technical governance (it does not override the maintainers on engineering decisions).

Godot has achieved significant financial sustainability through diversified community funding. Early on, it received large one-time grants (Microsoft and Epic Games provided grants with no strings attached in 2018–2019). To reduce reliance on sporadic big donations, Godot launched a Godot Development Fund for recurring user and corporate contributions. This is essentially a donation subscription platform (akin to Patreon but self-hosted to avoid fees) where individuals and companies can pledge monthly support. By 2023, this fund already attracted many sponsors, enabling Godot to hire several core developers full-time). Godot also participates in industry programs (for example, Google’s Season of Docs, etc.) and is now under the umbrella of large non-profit fiscal hosts (Software Freedom Conservancy previously, now its own foundation in Netherlands). All funding is transparent – the project regularly publishes breakdowns of income and expenses, and because it’s a non-profit, it reinvests everything into development or community events, a similar ethos to Ghost, though structured differently. The sustainability strategy is effectively “by the users, for the users”: the community that benefits from Godot (indie game devs, some game studios) is asked to financially support it so it can remain independent and free. This has been successful – Godot’s user community has grown exponentially, and even a small fraction donating is enough to cover core costs. They deliberately avoid venture capital or getting acquired by a corporation, because financial independence “empowers us to prioritize the needs and interests of our community and the open source principles at our core”. Notably, a company was formed (W4 Games) by Godot’s leaders to provide commercial services around Godot, but that operates separately, and Godot’s governance and funding remain community centric.

Godot is released under the MIT License – a permissive license with no copyleft requirements. This was a strategic choice to maximize adoption: game developers can use Godot with zero obligations, and even console platform code (which is proprietary) can be integrated without license conflict. MIT license also means companies can use Godot in commercial products without fear, encouraging industry usage (which in turn leads some companies to sponsor Godot financially). The risk of permissive licensing is that someone could fork Godot and make a closed product – but given the pace of the official project and the strength of the community, that hasn’t happened in a way that harms Godot. Instead, the open license helped it become a standard “public good” in game dev, with contributors comfortable donating code without copyleft concerns. Godot’s assets (documentation, demos) are also open (various permissive/Creative Commons licenses).

Godot’s journey from a tiny project to a leading OSS game engine offers multiple lessons. First, building a donor community can sustain large-scale development: by engaging users early (via Patreon, etc.) and proving the value of the engine, Godot managed to hire talent without selling out. They highlight that recurring user-funded contributions and sponsorships reduce dependence on large one-time corporate grants, increasing financial freedom. For LiteFarm, cultivating user and stakeholder donations (even small but steady) could provide stability beyond initial grants.

Second, Godot’s governance shows the balance between community input and strong leadership. They learned to be very open about process (e.g. explaining why certain user-suggested features are declined, focusing on solving common needs) to prevent frustration, all while keeping a coherent vision. Communication is key – something LiteFarm should prioritize if community contributions grow.

Third, Godot leveraged its permissive license to integrate into the industry, which then creates a virtuous funding loop (companies that use it often become sponsors). LiteFarm might analogously consider how an open approach could lead to support from agtech companies or governments that adopt it. A challenge Godot faced is scaling governance: with the explosion of popularity, they had to handle more feature requests than they could review. They implemented a proposal vetting system to manage this, essentially filtering by community interest and maintainer capacity. The lesson is to put processes in place so community ideas are heard but also evaluated for alignment and resources. Finally, Godot underscores the principle that financial independence = governance independence: by not relying on any single corporate sponsor or owner, they avoid external pressures on project direction. This is a compelling model for an academic project like LiteFarm looking to stand on its own: diversify support (lots of small backers rather than one big one) and institutionalize the project as a non-profit or foundation to hold funds and hire developers in service of the community.

Matrix

www.matrix.org

Pathway B.1

Matrix is open-source communication protocol that allows different messaging apps to talk to each other. It is governed by the Matrix.org Foundation, a UK non-profit Community Interest Company that acts as a neutral custodian of the Matrix protocol. The Foundation was created to ensure open, balanced decision-making and to prevent any single company from controlling Matrix’s evolution. It oversees the Matrix specification through an open governance process: a Spec Core Team and working groups accept proposals (MSCs) from the community, and decisions are made transparently based on technical merit and consensus. The Foundation’s Board (with elected members from the community and stakeholders) defines Matrix’s manifesto and values and steers the project’s strategic direction. As Matthew Hodgson (co-founder) noted when launching the Foundation, the goal is to “maintain and evolve the spec in a manner which benefits the whole ecosystem without privileging any individual commercial concerns.” In practice, this means key protocol changes or governance matters go through community review and Foundation approval. The importance of a neutral guardian is clear: without it, the Matrix ecosystem would likely fragment or fall under the influence of the largest corporate player. Thus, the Matrix.org Foundation’s open governance has been crucial in keeping Matrix decentralized and community driven.

Sustaining Matrix has been an ongoing challenge, and the project is exploring diversified funding strategies. Initially, much of Matrix’s development was bankrolled by the startup Element (formerly New Vector), which contributed over 95% of core code over the years. Today, the Matrix.org Foundation raises funds to support the protocol’s development and infrastructure. It accepts donations and has introduced paid membership tiers for companies and supporters. Despite nearly doubling revenue in 2024 to about $561,000, the Foundation faced an annual budget of roughly $1.2 million as it took on more operational costs from Element. This led to a deficit, prompting an urgent fundraising appeal in early 2025 to cover a $100k shortfall and keep key services running. The Foundation has had to prioritize spending on critical areas like spec maintenance and Trust & Safety, even scaling back non-essentials (e.g. discontinuing some Matrix.org-run bridges) to stretch its budget. Going forward, Matrix is pursuing grants and additional corporate sponsorships to become financially sustainable. The community’s growth is also seen as a sustainability driver: the more organizations adopt Matrix, the more incentive those stakeholders have to fund the core infrastructure. In summary, Matrix is transitioning from reliance on a single vendor to a broader funding base via its non-profit Foundation, but it remains at a crossroads where increased external support is needed to fully sustain the project’s open-source ecosystem.

Matrix’s approach to licensing balances open collaboration with the realities of commercial use. The Matrix protocol specifications are open and free for anyone to implement, and most reference implementations started under permissive licenses (Apache 2.0). However, in late 2023 there was a significant licensing shift for Matrix’s core server software. Element – the company that develops the popular Synapse and Dendrite Matrix servers – announced it would relicense future versions from Apache 2.0 to AGPLv3 (Affero GPL). This change was intended to enforce reciprocity: companies packaging Matrix’s server code into proprietary offerings must now open their modifications or purchase a commercial license. Element’s CEO, Matthew Hodgson, explained that some integrators and defense firms were “picking up our open source, selling it for huge amounts, and not contributing anything back,” so moving to AGPLv3 compels them to contribute code or funds. The Matrix.org Foundation itself was initially hesitant about adding a Contributor License Agreement (CLA) and stronger copyleft, preferring pure open source without additional encumbrances. But the Foundation recognized that using AGPLv3 is far better than a closed-source model if it leads to greater investment in the ecosystem. End-user Matrix clients (like Element) remain open source (some under Apache, some GPL) and the protocol remains open-standard. In effect, Matrix’s licensing strategy has evolved: the specification and most client libraries stay permissively licensed for broad adoption, while the homeserver components adopted AGPLv3 to ensure the sustainability of open development. This nuanced licensing model is a direct response to the project’s growth and aims to protect against “freeloading” by encouraging either code contributions or financial support from commercial users.

Matrix’s journey highlights several challenges common in open-source infrastructure projects, along with hard-earned lessons. One major challenge has been sustainability: even with over 115 million users on the network by late 2023, the project struggled to convert that popularity into financial support. The Foundation learned that relying on a single company’s goodwill (Element) left the project vulnerable; thus it has been building a more resilient funding model and making tough decisions like cutting non-critical services to survive. Another challenge is governance in a commercially active ecosystem. Matrix had to ensure its protocol development remains vendor-neutral and open – the Foundation’s existence and the open spec process address this by preventing any one vendor from dictating the roadmap. The licensing saga in 2023 provided a key lesson: a 100% permissive licensing approach can enable wide adoption, but it may allow large players to profit without giving back. By shifting to AGPL for server code, Matrix illustrated an alternative path to sustainability for open-source projects: “obligating some of the other players to also play in open source… or find a different way to fund the underlying projects.”. This move, while controversial, underscores a lesson that open-source projects must sometimes adapt their licensing to protect the community’s long-term health. Matrix also encountered technical and social challenges – from scaling the network to managing abuse – which required investment in community safety teams and performance improvements, stretching resources thin.

The project’s recent crossroads moment (with an urgent funding appeal in 2025) taught the community the importance of outreach and transparency. By openly publishing its financial situation and appealing for help, the Matrix Foundation mobilized public support and raised awareness about how critical its work is. For LiteFarm, Matrix’s experience demonstrates the need for a clear governance structure, proactive funding efforts (including diversified revenue), and careful consideration of licensing to balance openness with fairness – all while keeping the community engaged and informed.

LandPKS (Land Potential Knowledge System)

 

www.landpotential.org

Pathway B.2

LandPKS is a free, open-source mobile app that helps farmers, ranchers, and land managers identify soils, monitor soil health, and track vegetation. It started as a USDA/ARS-led open-source tool for land assessment. Initially, governance was within a government research unit. In 2022, facing the end of its USDA phase, the founder (Jeff Herrick) sought a new home for sustainability and community growth. He partnered with Tech Matters, a non-profit tech incubator, to rebuild LandPKS as a modern OSS suite and integrate it into Tech Matters’ Terraso platform. Now governance is handled by Tech Matters in collaboration with the original team – effectively transferring stewardship from a government agency to a non-profit.

Decisions on features and strategy are made by the Terraso/LandPKS team at Tech Matters, with input from the user community (conservationists, land managers). This move created a clearer community-centered governance, as Tech Matters’ mission aligns with open, user-driven development versus internal government priorities. Under USDA, LandPKS was grant-funded. Its transition involved philanthropic funding and non-profit support. Tech Matters, backed by donors, provides engineering resources and infrastructure to LandPKS. The project’s sustainability now leverages Tech Matters’ model: offering the software as a public good (free apps) while seeking grants and partnerships for support. By being part of Terraso (a suite of open tools for land management), LandPKS can share funding sources and user communities. The plan is to scale to “the next ten million users” with open-source efficiency. This case shows an “OSS exit” via non-profit acquisition – ensuring continuous support without commercialization. LandPKS remains open source (free apps for Android/iOS, with code available). Tech Matters is committed to open-source principles; the new LandPKS app suite is being built under permissive licenses. The data and content LandPKS produces are also openly accessible, aligning with its origin in public sector research. A major lesson is the importance of finding the right institutional home. LandPKS avoided a shutdown by moving out of academia/government into an organization designed to sustain technology for good: Tech Matters focuses on open, scalable tech for social good, matching LandPKS’s ethos. LiteFarm, similarly at a university, can consider such a hand-off to a mission-driven non-profit or coalition as an exit strategy. The pitfall to avoid during the transition was letting a project wither after initial funding ends; LandPKS solved this through an “exit” that kept it open and community centric.

AgStack

agstack.org

Pathway B.2

AgStack is an open-source digital infrastructure project acting like a foundational "operating system" for agriculture that developers, researchers, and agronomists can use to build farming, food tracking, and AI-native applications. It operates under the Linux Foundation’s governance framework, which provides a neutral structure for decision-making. As a Linux Foundation project, AgStack has a community-led governance model with an Executive Director (Sumer Johal) and a governing board drawn from its member organizations. Technical decisions are made openly by contributors and overseen by community committees similar to a Technical Steering Committee, following Linux Foundation best practices. All code and project resources are governed by the AgStack community in an open manner within the Linux Foundation, ensuring no single entity controls the project. This open governance approach aims to balance input from industry, academia, and government stakeholders for the benefit of the whole agriculture ecosystem.

AgStack’s sustainability model relies on support from its consortium of founding members and sponsors, coordinated through the Linux Foundation. At launch, AgStack brought together major agtech companies, research institutions, and non-profits as members – for example, HPE, IBM, the FarmOS community, universities, and more joined as contributors. These members likely provide funding via membership dues and in-kind contributions to support AgStack’s core infrastructure and coordination (a typical Linux Foundation funding approach). Being a Foundation project also positions AgStack to receive grants or contributions tied to open digital infrastructure for agriculture. While specific financials aren’t public, AgStack’s inclusion of diverse stakeholders and Linux Foundation backing help it tap into multiple funding streams (industry sponsorships, collaborative grants, etc.), aiming for long-term sustainability of its open data platforms. The Linux Foundation’s support provides administrative resources and a trusted reputation, reducing overhead for the project and attracting ongoing investment in AgStack’s mission.

AgStack emphasizes permissive open-source licensing to maximize adoption and collaboration. All contributed code is made available under an open-source license and uses permissively licensed tools. This means AgStack’s software components are likely released under licenses such as Apache 2.0 or similar, allowing broad reuse and integration. The focus on permissive licenses aligns with AgStack’s goal of providing “free, re-usable, open” digital infrastructure for agriculture. By avoiding restrictive licensing, AgStack encourages companies and researchers to build on its datasets and frameworks without fear of vendor lock-in. Additionally, as AgStack integrates existing open projects (like Kubernetes, Hyperledger, etc.), it inherits their licensing, which skews permissive or Apache-style in many cases. This licensing strategy lowers barriers to entry and invites contributions, while the Linux Foundation’s IP policies ensure contributors and users are protected in a neutral IP commons.

AgStack faces the challenge of uniting a traditionally fragmented agriculture tech sector under a common open platform. One lesson from its early efforts is the importance of collaboration over reinventing the wheel. For instance, AgStack partnered with LF Edge’s EdgeX Foundry project to leverage existing IoT edge technology instead of building from scratch – a move that “gives us the opportunity to leapfrog our IoT/edge efforts by several years.” This showed how cross-project collaboration can accelerate development and address resource constraints. Another challenge is ensuring broad community buy-in across industry and smallholder farmers alike for data sharing; AgStack’s strategy has been to operate as a neutral facilitator so that stakeholders trust the platform. Early lessons emphasize that open data infrastructure can address inefficiencies in agriculture, but it requires cultivating a diverse contributor base. AgStack’s consortium approach and Linux Foundation governance have helped avoid dominance by any single player, addressing a key risk in open-source initiatives. As a result, AgStack is gradually establishing itself as critical “digital public infrastructure” for agriculture. 

Open Data Kit (ODK)

​

www.getodk.org​

Pathway B.3

ODK is an open-source suite of software that lets users build forms to collect data, even without an internet connection. It began as a university project and evolved into a community-managed project with formal governance. It formed a Project Management Committee and a Technical Steering Committee (TSC) of diverse community contributors in 2018. The TSC (elected/appointed from active contributors) oversees technical roadmaps and release decisions, operating by consensus. This meritocratic governance opened up what was once led by a few academic founders into a broader community effort. Decisions are discussed on public forums and calls, making the process transparent and inclusive. ODK is sustained by a consortium of users and organizations. It doesn’t sell the software, so funding comes from grants (e.g. Digital Square, Gates Foundation in earlier years), consulting firms (like Nafundi or Ona) that implement ODK and contribute back, and an Open Collective for community donations. Many NGOs and governments use ODK, and some sponsor feature development or contribute developer time. The community pooling of resources is a strength: “different groups are able to pool limited resources and share the work,” and no single organization controls the project. Volunteers worldwide write code, create docs, and help on forums. Red Cross, for example, both uses ODK widely and has staff on the TSC, and also financially supports improvements). This mix of in-kind and monetary contributions keeps ODK evolving.

ODK is Apache 2.0 licensed (permissive), encouraging broad adoption and integration. This licensing aided its spread in low-resource contexts and allowed a community ecosystem (including spinoffs like ODK-X). The open license also enabled third-party service providers to build businesses around ODK without violating licenses – though it meant no direct license revenue for the project. ODK’s journey illustrates graduating from a founder-led project to community governance. That brought scalability (more contributors and maintainers) but required defining processes (the TSC charter) and managing a large user community’s needs.

A lesson for LiteFarm is the value of forming a governance committee early to distribute decision-making. ODK also shows that even without selling software, an OSS project can thrive through a support ecosystem, but it must actively cultivate that community of contributors, service providers, and donors. Transparency (open forums, public roadmaps) has been critical to ODK’s community trust and longevity.

 

Home Assistant

www.home-assistant.io​

Pathway B.4

Nabu Casa Home Assistant is an open-source home automation platform that started as a solo project and grew into a large community. Governance is characterized by a benevolent dictator model with a supporting organization. Paulus Schoutsen, the founder, leads the project’s direction (he remains the final arbiter on major decisions), but he’s backed by a core team of maintainers who oversee various integrations and components. They practice an informal meritocracy: contributors who consistently provide good code or support often become integration maintainers with commit access. The project values community input highly – feature requests and RFCs are discussed on their forums and GitHub, and many new integrations are community- contributed. However, to maintain quality and security (important in home automation), core developers review and approve all changes. To give the project a formal home, the founders set up the Open Home Foundation (a non-profit) which holds the trademarks and IP, and a for-profit company Nabu Casa, Inc. which the founders also run to handle cloud services and funding. Decision-making on the open-source project remains independent of any corporate agenda because Nabu Casa has no outside investors and is entirely user-funded. This means Paulus and team can prioritize features that benefit users and align with privacy (local control). Therefore, governance is centralized (one leadership team) but deeply informed by a passionate community – a large volunteer contributor base and user community influence priorities, and the project’s open nature keeps the leaders accountable to the community (if they veered off mission, the community would object and potentially fork, though that hasn’t been needed).

Home Assistant achieved sustainability through a paid cloud service and user subscriptions. In 2018, the founders created Nabu Casa and launched Home Assistant Cloud, a $5/month optional subscription that provides convenience features (like secure remote access and voice assistant integration). This service is entirely optional – the core software is free and fully functional without it – but many users opt in to support development (and for the added ease-of-use). This model generated enough revenue that Nabu Casa now employs several full-time developers for Home Assistant. Critically, Nabu Casa has no investors and is solely funded by its users, which means all revenue goes into the product and community (and they aren’t pressured to increase profits at the expense of users). The company provides the infrastructure and pays salaries, effectively acting as a commercial backbone for the OSS project while being aligned (the same people lead both). Home Assistant also occasionally gets corporate partnerships, e.g. hardware companies sponsoring integration development, or the partnership with Silicon Labs for Zigbee support, but these are structured to improve the platform for all rather than exclusivity. Another smaller revenue stream is the “Works with Home Assistant” certification program for IoT devices, which Nabu Casa runs – fees from that also support the project. The result is a virtuous cycle where around 100k users paying a few dollars monthly can sustain development for millions of users who use it for free. This community patronage model has allowed Home Assistant to grow explosively without selling out or resorting to ads.

Home Assistant is open-source under the Apache 2.0 License. This permissive license was chosen to encourage broad integration of third-party hardware/software and to not discourage commercial use. It ensures that anyone can embed Home Assistant or extend it without GPL concerns (important for hardware vendors or app developers who might integrate it). The project’s ethos is strongly pro-privacy and open standards, so keeping it Apache 2.0 fits – it maximizes compatibility with other open or closed components in a smart home. The only notable exception is some of the Home Assistant Cloud code is closed-source (since that’s part of Nabu Casa’s value-add), but that doesn’t affect the open source core. Most user contributions are to the core and integrations, all under Apache license, enabling an ecosystem of plugins and add-ons (which can be MIT, Apache, etc. as well). This licensing approach, combined with the non-profit foundation owning the trademarks, prevents any one company (besides Nabu Casa, which is community-aligned) from hijacking the project while keeping it open for collaboration.

Home Assistant illustrates a path where an open-source project can remain community-governed yet financially self-sustaining via a for-profit arm that serves the community. For LiteFarm, a similar approach could be: provide a hassle-free hosted LiteFarm or premium support under a company, use that income to fund development, and keep the project open and community-centric.

Key lessons include:

●               Users will pay for convenience and to support a project they believe in. By not handicapping the free product but offering convenience (like one-click remote access), Home Assistant turned a fraction of users into paying subscribers, essentially acting as patrons.

●               Stay mission-focused: Nabu Casa explicitly has no external investors and a charter to focus on privacy and user benefit, ensuring the funding mechanism doesn’t compromise governance. This is a delicate balance – many projects struggle when money enters, but Home Assistant’s team being the same people on both OSS and company side avoids conflict of interest.

●               Don’t be afraid to form a company. There can be hesitation in OSS to commercialize, but this case shows that a community-informed company (sometimes dubbed a “COSS” – commercial open-source software entity) can greatly accelerate a project. Since launching Nabu Casa, Home Assistant’s pace and polish have improved, benefiting all users (paid or not). A challenge in this model is making sure the community trusts the company – Home Assistant did this by extreme transparency (Paulus regularly shares subscriber numbers, revenue is known to fund specific hires, etc.) and by delivering on promises (features that require cloud are minimal and optional). Another challenge is scaling support – the community forums handle most support, which works because the user base is enthusiastic; this may need attention as any paid user might expect support (Nabu Casa does handle Cloud-related support though). For LiteFarm, the scale might be smaller, but even a modest cloud subscription or SaaS offering to organizations could provide a steady funding stream. Home Assistant’s success ultimately teaches that community-first does not mean money-last – you can harness community power to generate funds in a way that feeds back into the community through better software.

Odoo

www.odoo.com

Pathway B.4

Odoo is a popular open-source ERP/CRM suite that began as TinyERP/OpenERP. Governance is corporate-led with community participation. Odoo S.A. (a private company founded by Fabien Pinckaers), drives the roadmap and development of the core platform. The company’s leadership makes final decisions on features and releases, balancing community feedback with commercial priorities. Alongside, there is an Odoo Community Association (OCA) – a non-profit that organizes volunteer contributors and maintains community modules. The OCA follows a project steering committee model for those add-ons, but it does not control the main Odoo project (it collaborates by contributing improvements and localizations which Odoo S.A. may merge).

In essence, Odoo’s governance is a hybrid: a benevolent dictatorship (the company) for core, and a meritocratic community for extensions. This model has kept development fast and consistent (single vision) but occasionally causes friction when community desires diverge from the company’s plans. Overall, Odoo S.A. has been responsive to community needs insofar as it benefits product adoption, but governance is not fully democratic – it’s guided by a commercial product management approach. Odoo is sustained by a commercial open-core model. The core Community Edition is open-source and free (LGPL v3 license), while Odoo’s Enterprise Edition includes additional proprietary modules and offers official support to paying customers. The company generates revenue from Enterprise subscriptions, cloud hosting, and partnerships (Odoo partners implement the software for clients and share revenue). This has proven highly successful: Odoo grew to tens of millions in revenue and a large user base globally, achieving profitability and rapid growth. They famously did not take big VC until very late, growing mostly on customer revenue. By having a dual offering, Odoo monetizes features desired by bigger customers (e.g. advanced accounting) while still engaging the open-source community with the free version. The community contributes many modules (there are thousands of third-party apps), which enhance Odoo’s ecosystem value for free – a sustainability boost. Odoo’s approach shows that selling proprietary add-ons or services on top of an OSS core can fund continual development. The risk is ensuring the open core remains robust so that the community stays engaged; Odoo has managed this by continuing to release a strong free core even as it expands commercial features.

Odoo used the AGPL v3 license for its open-source code until 2015, then moved to LGPL v3. This licensing shift was strategic: AGPL had required anyone providing Odoo as a service to open-source their changes, which discouraged third-party module developers who wanted to sell proprietary modules. By relicensing to LGPL (a weaker copyleft), Odoo enabled a plugin marketplace and proprietary modules without violating the license. This helped build a broader contributor ecosystem (freelance developers and software vendors could create Odoo apps for sale, encouraging investment in the platform). Odoo explained this as finding a better balance between freedom and business: it kept Odoo open-source (LGPL is OSI-approved but gave developers the legal security to get compensated for their extensions. Odoo’s Enterprise modules are under a proprietary license (customers get source access but cannot redistribute). This dual-license strategy (open core + commercial license for extras) is a classic open source business approach. It has been effective but requires discipline in partitioning what is free vs. paid.

Odoo’s case offers a contrast to purely community-run projects. It shows that having a single strong entity in charge can lead to fast product evolution and a clear business model – Odoo is arguably one of the most feature-rich OSS business softwares, due in part to heavy company investment. For LiteFarm, if a more commercial route is considered, Odoo provides a blueprint: an open-source base to gain adoption, and additional proprietary offerings to monetize. The lessons include: (1) License strategy matters: Odoo realized their copyleft license was limiting ecosystem growth and made the tough choice to go more permissive to foster a plugin economy.. LiteFarm should carefully choose a license that aligns with its desired level of community vs. commercial activity. (2) Community relationship: Odoo’s thriving community of module developers exists because the company actively supports them (through OCA, documentation, allowing their modules in the ecosystem). Even if governance is company-centric, respecting and enabling the community is crucial – e.g. Odoo accepts contributions and gives credit, and OCA provides a voice for community needs. A pitfall in this model is if the community feels second-class or if the open version lags too far behind enterprise; Odoo has had to manage this perception continuously. Another challenge is avoiding extractive behavior – Odoo mitigated this by ensuring the open core remains “most of the product” (for instance, when they open-sourced their proprietary CMS and e-commerce modules, they cited benefit to users and attracting more devs. The takeaway for LiteFarm is that commercial sponsorship can turbo-charge development, but one must balance it with community goodwill, possibly via an independent community org or clear delineation of what stays open.

farmOS

farmos.org

Pathway B.4

farmOS is a community-driven open-source farm management system. Governance has been intentionally structured to remain independent and community-centric. The project was founded by Michael Stenta, who acts as the BDFL (benevolent dictator for now) of the project, but he has explicitly kept farmOS separate from any single company influence. Decision-making happens in the open: a public forum and chat where farmers, developers, and researchers discuss features and priorities. Core maintainers (including the founder and a few active contributors) review code and decide on releases. In practice, Stenta and core devs guide the roadmap, but they do so based on user needs from the community and collaborative design – for example, features often originate from stakeholder requests (sponsored development) which are then generalized for all. There is no formal foundation; instead, farmOS relies on a light governance model where trust and shared mission keep everyone aligned. Recently, they’ve begun exploring more structured community governance (e.g. how to vote on use of donated funds, which indicates a gradual move toward committee-style governance as the community grows. Importantly, farmOS’s governance emphasizes that the project belongs to its users and contributors (“the project remained independent of any company”.

farmOS employs a mixed sustainability approach. The core idea was to avoid venture capital and instead support development through services and community support. The founder created Farmier, a separate business that offers hosted farmOS servers, custom development, and support contracts. Revenue from Farmier (paid by farmers and organizations using farmOS) funds a significant portion of core development. In essence, farmOS uses a “commercial support arm” model: the software is free, but those who need hosting or specific features pay Farmier, and that money feeds back into the open-source project. Additionally, sponsored development plays a big role – universities, non-profits, and farmers have paid to have certain features developed, which are then contributed to the public codebase. This contract work provides income to sustain the lead developer while improving farmOS for everyone (e.g. a university might fund a module for nutrient tracking, which gets added to core for all users). farmOS also accepts donations via Open Collective, gathering community contributions to fund maintenance tasks that don’t have direct sponsors. This allows even those who can’t contract features to support ongoing work (documentation, security updates, etc) in a transparent way. The combination of earned income and community crowdfunding has kept farmOS afloat and growing since its start, though the product itself may not be up to competitor/industry standards.

farmOS is released under the GNU General Public License (GPLv2) (like many Drupal-based projects, as farmOS v1 was built on Drupal). This strong copyleft license ensures that any modifications must be shared alike, protecting the community’s interest in improvements. It didn’t hinder adoption in its domain because users (small farmers, orgs) typically are not redistributing modified versions, just using the software. With farmOS 2.0 (now a standalone Python/JS stack), they have kept it open source (GPL or a similar copyleft license for consistency). The license choice reinforces independence: no company can take farmOS code and make a proprietary version without violating the license, which safeguards the project’s open future. It’s notable that despite having a commercial arm (Farmier), they did not create a proprietary “enterprise edition” – Farmier’s services use the same GPL farmOS code. This demonstrates commitment to true open-source principles.

farmOS provides a valuable model for LiteFarm as an open ag-tech project. Key lessons:

●               Independence: By structuring a companion service business (Farmier) rather than relying on a parent company or VC, farmOS kept control in the hands of its community-focused founder. LiteFarm could consider a similar approach (providing paid hosting/support) to fund development while keeping governance in the project community.

●               User-driven development: farmOS’s best features often came from paid real-world use cases. This ensures resources go into things farmers actually need, aligning sustainability with usefulness. It’s effectively a “sponsor a feature” model, which could work well in agricultural software where stakeholders might fund features like integrations or analytics that they require.

●               Community infrastructure: farmOS underscores that sustaining OSS is not just coding – it requires maintaining forums, documentation, demos, etc. The Open Collective donations specifically help pay for these communal needs in a transparent way. Encouraging even small donations ($2/mo) from users can cumulatively support the project’s health. A challenge farmOS faces is growing beyond the founder as the single full-time developer – this is a common bottleneck (the “bus factor”). They are mitigating it by building up community contributors and potentially hiring via grant funds if available. The lesson is to plan governance transitions as the project grows (e.g. a steering committee to eventually take collective leadership). Finally, farmOS highlights the importance of philosophy: it treats software as a “digital public good” akin to public infrastructure, which resonates with funders and users who then see value in contributing back. LiteFarm, having similar positioning as a public-good tool for sustainable farming, can leverage that mindset to attract a supportive community and ethical funding sources.

Are.na

www.are.na

Pathway C.2

Are.na (pronounced “Arena”) is a community-driven digital platform for collaborative curation of ideas. While not a traditional OSS project (its code is not fully open-source), it’s a relevant case of community-centric governance and sustainability. Are.na is a for-profit company with a strong community ethos. The co-founders deliberately avoided typical top-down growth; instead, they involve members in the platform’s evolution. In 2018, they decided to crowdfund investment from users so that members could become owners and share in governance aspects. Through an equity crowdfunding campaign, any user could buy a stake, aligning the company’s future with its community. This move was meant to ensure Are.na grows in line with user values and doesn’t sacrifice its mission for profit.

Day-to-day decisions (feature development, moderation policies) are made by the core team, but they are heavily influenced by community feedback and needs,  effectively a community-informed product governance. There’s no formal user council, but early adopters and paying members have a voice via forums and direct communication with the team. The governance priority is maintaining a calm, thoughtful space online, so policies (like no ads, no algorithms) are fixed to values which were set in consultation with the community.

Are.na’s sustainability model is membership-supported. It offers premium memberships (subscription fees) for individuals and teams, which provide additional features and also function as a way for users to support the platform. By 2018, this member revenue was on track to cover expenses, making the platform financially sustainable long-term. Equity crowdfunding brought in additional capital for development without resorting to venture capital. In essence, Are.na is funded by its users, both through subscriptions and as shareholder-investors. This approach means no reliance on advertising (which they refuse for ethical reasons) and no pressure from VCs to pursue hyper-growth. They have also received some grants in the art/tech space, but the core idea is “funded by those who benefit.” The community-ownership angle strengthens this: when users are literally investors, they have a stake in keeping the platform alive (similar to a cooperative mindset).

Are.na’s software isn’t fully open source; the front-end client was at least partially open as they leveraged open-source components and have shared some code, but the platform as a whole is proprietary to the company. However, all user-contributed content can be made public and shared, aligning with open knowledge principles. While not an OSS licensing strategy, Are.na instead employs a “data commons” strategy – users retain ownership of data and can export it. The choice to keep code closed might be due to wanting more control or lack of a broad developer contributor base. The trade-off is less outside contribution to development, but the team focuses on transparent operations and close communication rather than open-sourcing code. Are.na highlights a model where community and financial sustainability are deeply intertwined. 

For LiteFarm, this suggests exploring creative funding: e.g. community-supported agriculture has members invest upfront in a harvest – similarly, community-supported software could have users invest in the platform’s development for shared returns or influence. Are.na’s governance also shows the value of values alignment: they set out clear principles (no ads, no data exploitation) and built governance and funding around those, which attracted a like-minded user base who are willing to pay and invest. A potential pitfall in Are.na’s approach is scaling – as a small community-driven venture, growth is slower and requires a loyal niche. But if the aim is long-term resilience over rapid expansion, this can be a virtue. The platform’s survival thus far demonstrates that a committed community can sustain an independent tech platform when given ownership and when the product’s ethos resonates. Are.na’s users support it because it “feels like a garden, not a firehose” of social media. Are.na provides a real-world example of community governance in practice financially: prioritize user well-being over monetization, and invite those users to fund and even own a tool they love and find useful.

Red Hat (Linux Ecosystem)

www.redhat.com

Pathway C.3

Red Hat contributes heavily to the Linux kernel and related Open Source Software (OSS), but governance of Linux itself is a benevolent dictatorship/meritocracy under Linus Torvalds and a core maintainer team. Red Hat stewards its own distributions (Fedora community edition, Red Hat Enterprise Linux operating system (RHEL) enterprise edition) with upstream-first development. Major technical decisions for Linux are made on mailing lists by maintainers; Red Hat influences via employed maintainers but doesn’t control Linux governance outright.

Red Hat pioneered a commercial OSS model by selling subscriptions for support and services on open-source software. It built a profitable business (acquired by IBM for $34 billion in 2019) while keeping its software mostly open source. Revenue from enterprise customers sustains full-time developers and open-source contributions, proving OSS can be financially viable at scale, while also introducing new tensions between free community versions and paid offerings.

Red Hat’s Linux distribution is under a GPL license, as is the Linux kernel, ensuring source code availability. They leverage trademark and subscription terms to differentiate paid support vs. free rebuilds. In 2023 Red Hat controversially limited access to RHEL source updates, seen as “paywalling” their distro and drawing community backlash.  Fedora, the upstream source for RHEL, remains free and community-governed, balancing openness with business needs. A key lesson is the balance between community and commercial interests. Red Hat’s success shows that contributing to commons can drive profit (trust from customers that software will remain open). However, its 2023 policy change on RHEL source highlighted a governance pitfall: if a corporate stakeholder makes unilateral decisions perceived as against community interests, it can “split the open-source community”. For LiteFarm, Red Hat’s model shows the potential of service-based revenue on OSS, while underscoring the importance of transparency and community trust when monetizing open-source software.

Open Collective

​

www.opencollective.com​

Tool/Platform

Open Collective is a platform that provides financial tooling and fiscal hosting for open-source projects. It’s not a single OSS project but rather a service used by many projects (including some in this list like ODK, farmOS) to transparently manage money. The Open Collective platform itself is run by a company, OFI Technologies, and associated non-profit foundation OFiCo. Governance of the platform is in the company’s hands (deciding features for the website etc.), but importantly, it enables decentralized decision-making for each project’s funds. Each project (Collective) on the platform can have its own budget and makes decisions on how to spend funds through its core contributors who submit expenses, with everything logged openly. So, while Open Collective (the entity) is run in a more traditional way, its mission is to empower communities with financial governance tools. For example, a project like farmOS uses Open Collective to let the community view all income/expenses and approve expense payouts. The Open Source Collective 501(c)(6) (an umbrella under Open Collective) has its own board including OSS community members, which oversees fiscal sponsorships. Thus, governance is layered: the platform provides the rules (all transactions public; certain rules for fund use), and projects govern their own finances within that.

Open Collective, the company, sustains itself by charging a small platform fee on transactions and by offering paid services to Collectives, like fiscal hosting through the Open Source Collective or other host organizations. It essentially provides a “legal and financial toolbox” so groups don’t have to form their own entity. This has proven valuable – thousands of OSS projects use it to receive donations, sponsorships, and grants. Because all expenditures are public, sponsors feel confident, which encourages funding. For the projects on it, Open Collective serves as a fundraising conduit with full transparency. It doesn’t solve how to get money initially, but it makes managing and displaying that money easier, which can attract more backers. The Open Source Collective (OSC) fiscal host, which is part of the platform’s ecosystem, has funded its operations by retaining a host fee and through dedicated grants (it is itself a non-profit representing OSS interests). In short, Open Collective has established a sustainable service that in turn helps OSS projects become more sustainable by removing overhead and increasing trust in their fundraising.

The Open Collective platform’s code is partly open-source (certain components are on GitHub under permissive licenses), though some backend parts might be closed. The concept it popularized – open finance – does not depend on license but on policy: all budgets and expense details are openly viewable by anyone online. This norm of financial transparency is perhaps as important as software license in the context of governance. It changes the culture of funding from opaque to participatory. Many of the projects hosted are themselves open source (and they must specify an OSI-approved license to join OSC). Open Collective’s success underscores how infrastructure can support community governance. For LiteFarm, using a platform like this could ease the transition to a community-funded model – it would allow money to be handled transparently and collectively, which might make stakeholders (donors, grantmakers, volunteers) more willing to contribute. A lesson here is the power of transparency in sustainability: when a project can show exactly how funds are used (for servers, for a developer stipend, etc.), it builds trust and accountability.

Another takeaway is that many OSS projects don’t fail for lack of users, but lack of admin capacity; Open Collective addresses that by outsourcing the admin (handling taxes, invoices). One pitfall is that Open Collective itself is a centralized service: projects depend on it and its hosts to handle their money (which could be an issue if those hosts falter or if a project outgrows what the platform offers). However, the model is flexible: some projects eventually form their own foundation or company, but many may not need to because the collective model suffices. In summary, Open Collective provides a governance framework for finances: decisions about spending still need to be made by the community, but the platform makes executing and documenting those decisions far easier. LiteFarm could leverage this to practice community governance in a concrete way (e.g. allocate a travel budget for contributors, vote on it, and pay through the Collective). 

Drips

www.drips.network

Tool/Platform

Drips is an open source, community-driven funding platform for OSS that exemplifies a novel governance mechanism: collective funding lists. As a project, Drips is developed by a small team (with input from the community on GitHub), but what’s more relevant is how it enables governance of fund allocation. Using Drips, communities can create “Drip Lists” – each list is a group of projects and developers with assigned percentages, and a group of collaborators who vote on those allocations. This allows a community (like a DAO or a group of maintainers) to collectively decide which projects/people receive what share of a pooled fund. The governance of each Drip List is up to its creators – some use token-weighted voting, some simple consensus among a few core people. In essence, Drips provides tooling for democratic or collaborative funding decisions. The project itself is maintained openly (the code for the protocol and app is on GitHub, with core devs merging contributions). Major decisions about Drips software are made by its founding team at this stage, but as it’s a toolkit rather than a service, the “governance” is largely in the hands of users configuring their funding flows. Drips is a funding mechanism rather than a source of funds. It allows continuous streaming of funds (in crypto, specifically ERC-20 tokens) to open-source maintainers. Sustainability of Drips (the project) likely comes from grants (it aligns with web3 public goods funding initiatives) and possibly a future DAO. But sustainability of projects using Drips is enhanced by features like auto-splitting to dependencies: when a project receives funds through Drips and has listed its own open-source dependencies, a portion of its funds get automatically forwarded to those dependencies. This creates a cascading funding graph that can sustain lower-level libraries. Drips itself does not charge platform fees (it’s fee-less by design, which encourages maximum flow-through of support. Already, ecosystems like Ethereum Name Service (ENS) have used Drips to distribute grants to dozens of critical OSS libraries. Drips thus shifts the funding model from one-off grants to continuous support, making sustainability more predictable for maintainers. Drips is fully open source (the smart contracts and web app are Apache/MIT licensed). The ethos is to maximize openness and interoperability – it integrates with GitHub so any public repo can receive funds even before maintainers formally join. Being on Ethereum, it inherits an ethos of open code and transparency (all transactions are public on-chain). No proprietary components limit its use; communities are free to adopt and modify it for their needs.

Drips represents an emerging “funding DAO” approach that LiteFarm and the greater Open AgTech ecosystem could borrow from, especially the idea of embedding funding into the workflow of usage. One lesson is the importance of simplifying donor experience: Drips lets people support dozens of projects in one go, lowering friction to contribute to OSS. For LiteFarm, making it easy for supporters (e.g. agri-businesses, NGOs) to fund the project and its ecosystem (maybe via a similar pooled fund) could improve funding diversity. Governance-wise, Drips shows that decentralized, community-led allocation is possible – but it requires an engaged community with shared goals. A pitfall is that this reliance on crypto may limit the pool of donors to those comfortable with that technology, and market volatility can affect funding consistency. Also, creating the right governance rules for a Drip List (to ensure fairness and avoid conflict) is non-trivial and will require extensive discussions and design effort. The overarching lesson is that technology can facilitate more democratic funding of OSS (reducing dependence on single sponsors) for both individual projects and entire ecosystems with projects that are highly interoperable and reliant on one another, which could help LiteFarm tap a broader base of support if it builds a coalition of stakeholders willing to co-fund its maintenance in a transparent way.

Protocol Guild

www.protocolguild.org​

Tool/Platform

Protocol Guild is an initiative to fund Ethereum’s core developers via a collective, member-governed fund. It’s not software but an on-chain governance experiment, relevant as an OSS sustainability model. The Guild comprises around 100–200 Ethereum L1 researchers and developers. Its governance is internal and peer-driven: the members, who are the funding beneficiaries manage a registry smart contract that lists all eligible contributors and their weight (share) of funds. Membership criteria (who qualifies as a core contributor) are defined by an eligibility framework, and adding or removing members is done by rough consensus of existing members via github pull requests on a public repo. There is no external council or company in charge as it is a self-curating collective (“a commons of peers”). Importantly, donors have zero influence on how Ethereum is developed; Protocol Guild deliberately separates funding from technical decision-making (stewardship of Ethereum continues in existing forums like AllCoreDevs, independent of who gave money).

A governance challenge here is maintaining trust among members to add new ones fairly and manage the funds long-term; the Guild addresses this by incentive alignment (members share the pool, so they carefully vet new additions). Funding & Sustainability: Protocol Guild is funded by donations of protocol tokens and fees from the wider Ethereum ecosystem. Numerous projects (Layer2 networks, DeFi protocols, etc.) have pledged a portion of their token supplies (often 1%) to the Guild as a thank-you for Ethereum’s development. To date it has raised about $70M+ in crypto for long-term vesting to members. For example, the Guild received 500k UNI tokens, 200k ENS tokens, 3M ARB tokens, and others during a pilot. Funds are held in smart contracts and released over years to members. This provides a financial “safety net” for core developers, aligning their incentives to stay in the ecosystem.

Sustainability here is tied to the health of Ethereum projects – as long as major projects keep voluntarily contributing (and token values hold), the Guild can continue. It’s a novel model of ecosystem self-funding: those who benefit from the OSS (Ethereum) invest in its maintainers. There is no revenue or business model per se; it’s more akin to an endowment or collective insurance for public goods that is funded by profit-generating organizations reliant on the core technology the guild and its members maintain. Not a traditional software project, but Ethereum’s core software clients are open source under various licenses like GPLv3, Apache, MIT depending on the client.) The concept of Protocol Guild itself is implemented via open-source smart contracts and is documented openly. All rules and member lists are public on-chain, ensuring transparency. Essentially, the “license” here is the social contract that funds are for the commons. Donations are given without strings (legally, they’re gifts to the Guild contract).

Protocol Guild addresses the “tragedy of the commons” in OSS by formalizing long-term contributor incentives. A lesson for LiteFarm is the idea of getting stakeholders to invest in the core technology’s maintainers. In agriculture, that could mean forming a guild of core LiteFarm devs and asking organizations that rely on LiteFarm to commit funding through 1% donations back, yearly membership fees, etc. The Guild’s governance shows that self-governance among maintainers can work when carefully structured – members collectively curate their ranks and no single entity controls the funds. A potential pitfall is reliance on voluntary contributions and crypto market fluctuations – if the ecosystem’s enthusiasm or financial ability wanes, funding could drop. However, by decoupling funding from any one company or foundation, the Guild model provides resilience (funds come from a broad base of supporters). Also notable is the strict firewall between money and technical decisions. This preserves the integrity of governance on the project itself. For LiteFarm, even if not as formal, encouraging a consortium of beneficiaries to co-fund development (without controlling it) could be a powerful sustainability mechanism.
